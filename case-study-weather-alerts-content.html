The Moment

It’s Already Up by the Time You Walk Over

No one wrote it. No one was on shift. A background service caught the National Weather Service alert off the wire, wrote a branded, plain-English story, drew the map, and pushed it live—in the seconds it took to cross the room. When the storm changes, the same story updates itself. When it’s over, it marks itself lifted.

And none of it existed a few months ago. When FLX Local Media took over Finger Lakes Daily News in January 2026, the site had no weather section at all—not a forecast, not an alert, nothing. Every piece of this—the alert desk, the self-updating live-blogs, the town forecast pages, the search dominance—was built from a standing start this spring.

How It Works

The Same Official Feed—Just Faster to Our Audience

We don’t front-run the National Weather Service or replace anyone’s emergency alerting. We read the same public NWS alert feed that EAS and NOAA Weather Radio pull from—and surface it on our own site the instant it changes, before it has finished cycling onto the airwaves.

📡
Polls every 60 seconds
A cloud worker checks the NWS active-alerts feed across 19 Finger Lakes zones and counties, around the clock, and fingerprints the state so it never double-posts.
api.weather.gov · every minute
⚠
Reads the whole ladder
Watches, Warnings, Advisories, Statements—every tier. That’s why the storm threat shows on the site as a Watch, hours before there’s a Warning for anyone to broadcast.
Watch → Warning → Advisory
📝
Writes it in our voice
Each alert becomes a branded, plain-English story with a live radar map—county names, timing, what to do—sourced strictly from the NWS text, no embellishment.
Branded, fact-checked
🔄
Updates & lifts itself
One evolving live-blog per storm: it extends as the warning extends, stacks a new timestamped entry each change, and flips to LIFTED when it clears. Nobody has to remember to take it down.
Zero staff touches
The important guardrail: the system is built to report on warnings, not events. It restates what the National Weather Service said and links the full official alert text—it never invents a threat. Smart and fast, not reckless.

Why It Matters

Weather Is the Moment Local Media Is For

Severe weather is the single biggest reason people turn to a local station’s website and social—and it’s exactly when a lean newsroom is slammed or off the clock. Turning the free government alert feed into instant, on-brand, self-updating coverage means you own the storm story instead of losing that audience to TV or weather.com. We built the whole thing from a standing start in a few months this year—proof it’s a realistic play, not a moonshot.

Free input, owned audience

The NWS alert feed is public and license-free. The cost isn’t data or talent—it’s the plumbing that turns a warning into a branded post the instant it’s issued, and takes it down the instant it clears.

A sponsorable franchise

Always-on weather coverage—alerts, the live-blog, the email signup beside it—is a natural “Weather brought to you by ___” package. Automated public service that funds itself, not a cost center.
